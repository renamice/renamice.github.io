Train to Pakistan

Author: Khushwant Singh

The abilit of us humans to unite is an amazing sight to behold, but our ability to separate, or rather unite against, is equally terrifying.

Author guides us through the events of a village that was torn apart following the India-Pakistan split. The people in the village, even though not wanting anything to do with the seperation, people that just wanted peace, were forced into submission by the two governing bodies; bodies that forced them against their wants, in the name of helping them.

Later, the area became so much terryfing to be at that the crime which took place at the start didn’t even feel like it held any candle to the true horrors that was waiting to unfold in that area. Horrors against humanity, so great that, it may not have even been humans that did all that.

Protagonists, however, couldn’t have been any less similar, i.e. if they can even be called protagonists. They feel just like the rest of the cast, people victim to the time and place. One educated, one not.

You may have the biggest heart, the best plans, and the best visions for a bright future; all, however, mean nothing when you stop breating. The world forgets you and it moves on as if you were never even present in the first place.
This viewpoint may best summerize the attitude that Babuji developed towards the end, an educated man who lived in the UK prior to taking a train towards an unfortunate station. Although looking for political fame and prestige, his thoughts were shot down after witnessing what lack of proper order can look like, it wasn’t the UK anymore, it was a place near around the borders; a place where law wasn’t based on any constitution, but rather, the mood of the authority.

Jugga, on the other hand, marches towards, knowing fully well, his death. The time spent sharing prison with Babuji, and even the same cell for quite some time, had done nothing to cultivate a life first mindset as it did for Babuji. It is questionable why he chose to go down the way hi did. As per others’ descriptions, it wasn’t even possible for him to have even conceived, let alone acted, such “heroic” deed. But since humans are a complicated bunch, he too could have indeed changed, just like Babuji. Perhaps it was for love, after all, he was a simple man who let out his emotions with little to no constraints.

A highlight could be, how conflicts never actually solve anything. They just breed vultures that are egar to abandon their humanity, their ethics, and their morals, all in the name of patriotism or pro-(some ideology), and use that to justify themselves.
Conflicts just bring out the monster in us that we accuse our opposition of being; not even realising that it has long changed from:
“us” vs. “monster”, to
“monster” vs. “monster”.
